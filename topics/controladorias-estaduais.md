# Controladorias estaduais — CGE and equivalents

State-level controle interno bodies: the analogues of the federal
CGU for state administrations. Responsible for auditing state-funded
programs, running PAD of state civil servants, applying `LAC`
(Lei Anticorrupção) against firms at the state level, and
coordinating LAI implementation and transparency portals within the
state Executivo.

For the federal CGU, see `cgu-controle-interno.md`. For TCEs (external
control), see `tribunais-contas.md`. For state MPs, see
`ministerio-publico.md`. For state police, see `policias-estaduais.md`.

**Topics / keywords**: CGE, Controladoria-Geral do Estado, controle
interno estadual, PAD estadual, Lei Anticorrupção estadual, PAR,
acordo de leniência estadual, ouvidoria estadual, transparência
ativa, LAI estadual, CONACI, controladoria municipal, CGM.

**Snapshot as of 2026**: institutional framework is young (most
CGEs created post-2000; many post-`LAC` 2013). Cross-state variation
in capacity, autonomy, and activity is large, and comparable
aggregate data is scarce.

---

## 1. Constitutional and legal basis

Unlike the MP (`CF.127`), the Judiciário (`CF.92`), or the TCE
(`CF.75`), **the Constitution does not require states to maintain
a controladoria**. State controle interno flows from:

- **`CF.74`**: requires each branch of each federative entity to
  maintain "sistema de controle interno" articulated with the
  external control (TCU/TCE). Does not specify organ form.
- **`CF.37`**: principles of public administration (legality,
  impessoalidade, moralidade, publicidade, eficiência) — state
  administrations must organize internal control to enforce these.
- **`LAC` (Lei 12.846/2013)** Art. 8: attributes competence for
  administrative proceeding against firms to the "autoridade máxima
  de cada órgão ou entidade dos Poderes Executivo, Legislativo e
  Judiciário" at federal, state, and municipal levels. Triggered the
  creation or strengthening of state CGEs to handle corporate
  accountability.
- Each state's own **Constituição Estadual** and organizational laws
  define the CGE, its competence, and its subordination.

## 2. Organization

### Naming variation

CGEs operate under different labels across states:

- **Controladoria-Geral do Estado (CGE)** — most common (SP, MG, RJ,
  BA, GO, MT, and others).
- **Auditoria-Geral do Estado (AGE)** — historical RS form.
- **Secretaria da Transparência / Fazenda / Controle** — some states
  embed controle interno within a fiscal or control secretaria
  (weaker institutional profile).

Municipal analogues are **Controladorias-Gerais do Município (CGM)**,
present in larger cities (CGM-Rio, CGM-SP) but rare in small
municipalities. See state/municipal-specific files if added.

### Institutional status

Two broad models of CGE organization:

- **Secretaria de estado** (vinculada ao governador): politically
  subordinate; director is a cargo de confiança with no fixed
  mandate. Most states.
- **Órgão autônomo** with fixed-term direção: greater insulation
  from political cycles; São Paulo's CGE-SP (created by LC
  1.361/2021) is the leading example, with director appointed for
  a fixed term following legislative approval.

Both models remain subordinate to the Poder Executivo — none have
the constitutional autonomy of the MP or the judiciary.

### CONACI

- **CONACI — Conselho Nacional de Controle Interno**: voluntary
  cross-state coordination body. Publishes aggregate reports and
  coordinates technical standards across state controladorias.
- Operational rather than regulatory; CONACI does not bind members.

## 3. Instruments

CGEs deploy instruments that mirror the federal CGU (see
`cgu-controle-interno.md` SS2), adapted for state jurisdiction:

### Auditoria and fiscalização

- **Auditoria de programas estaduais**: thematic audit of state
  spending (education funds, health funds, obras).
- **Fiscalização de convênios**: state transfers to municipalities
  audited by the CGE when state money is involved.
- **No random-audit program analogous to the federal PFS**. Some
  states have introduced risk-based selection models, but the
  as-good-as-random lottery design never spread from CGU to CGEs.

### PAD estadual

- CGE (or a designated corregedoria) runs **Processo Administrativo
  Disciplinar** against state civil servants for infrações
  funcionais, following each state's estatuto dos servidores plus
  the federal-mirrored procedure of Lei 8.112/1990 where state law
  is silent.
- Sanctions: advertência, suspensão, demissão, cassação de
  aposentadoria. Cannot impose criminal or civil sanctions —
  separate tracks.

### PAR — Processo Administrativo de Responsabilização (`LAC`)

- The state-level application of `LAC.6` and `LAC.8`: CGE (or the
  designated autoridade máxima) investigates and sanctions firms
  for corruption against the state/municipal administration.
- Sanctions: multa (0.1%–20% of gross revenue), publicação
  extraordinária; judicial action for dissolução and other severe
  measures requires MP/AGU-PGE.
- Cross-state variation in how many PARs are actually concluded is
  large. Many states maintain the legal competence but produce few
  concluded processos.

### Acordo de leniência estadual

- Firms can negotiate leniência with the state CGE under `LAC.16` at
  state level. Formally available in all states that have structured
  their CGE accordingly.
- **Jurisdictional conflict with CGU**: the same firm may face
  parallel acordos with federal CGU (for federal contracts) and one
  or more state CGEs (for state contracts). Legal coordination is
  thin; the AGU/PGEs mediate in practice.
- **Double-jeopardy concern**: a firm that signs with CGU may still
  face PAR by CGEs for facts touching state administration, and
  vice versa. STF has not definitively resolved the scope of
  *ne bis in idem* across federative levels (see
  `anticorrupcao-penal.md` §8 on cross-regime sanctioning).

### Ouvidoria and LAI

- **Ouvidoria-Geral do Estado**: receives citizen complaints; often
  housed within the CGE.
- **LAI estadual**: CGE typically functions as state-level appellate
  authority for LAI requests within the Executivo (parallel to
  CMRI at federal level under `LAI.35`). State-specific procedures
  vary.

## 4. Cross-state variation

CGE capacity and activity vary substantially. Empirical concerns
for comparative research:

- **Staff size**: large states have hundreds of auditores and
  procuradores; small states have a skeleton team.
- **Budget**: not systematically reported.
- **Autonomy**: depends on the institutional model (secretaria vs.
  órgão autônomo) and on political conjuncture.
- **Activity metrics**: PARs concluded, acordos de leniência
  signed, PADs initiated — none have a single authoritative
  cross-state source. CONACI aggregates some indicators but coverage
  is incomplete.

### Notable states

- **São Paulo**: CGE-SP (LC 1.361/2021) — fixed-term direção,
  significant PAR activity, structured risk-based audit program.
- **Minas Gerais**: CGE-MG — active since 2003; large auditing
  team; coordinates with TCE-MG and MPE-MG.
- **Rio de Janeiro**: CGE-RJ — politically volatile, multiple
  scandals involving controle interno itself during the 2010s.

Several smaller states maintain CGEs primarily as a legal formality
with limited operational capacity.

## 5. Research design implications

### Data access

- **No cross-state standardized dataset** of CGE activity. Each CGE
  publishes its own annual reports and portals with varying detail.
- **LAI requests** can extract activity data but are state-by-state.
- **CONACI** is the closest thing to a clearinghouse but aggregates
  at a high level.
- **PARs and acordos de leniência** involve firm confidentiality
  protections that further limit public data.

### Selection into CGE action

- Non-random: responds to representações, denúncias, audit priorities,
  and political context. Selection effects are similar to but more
  politically embedded than at CGU level.
- **Lack of a random-audit analogue to PFS** means CGEs cannot
  anchor causal-inference designs in the way CGU audits did for
  Ferraz--Finan.

### Overlap with TCE

- The state-level controle interno (CGE) and controle externo (TCE)
  run parallel. A single irregularidade can generate:
  - CGE PAD of the responsible servant.
  - TCE processo of specific act or the annual contas.
  - MP improbidade ACP.
  - Criminal prosecution via PC or PF.
- Coordination is loose; the same facts can produce inconsistent
  outcomes across these tracks.

## 6. Key statutes

| Instrument | Scope |
|---|---|
| `CF.37`, `CF.74` | Principles of public administration; controle interno mandate |
| `LAC` (Lei 12.846/2013) | Corporate liability — applies to states via `LAC.8` |
| `LAI` (Lei 12.527/2011) | LAI — state analogues under each state's decreto regulamentador |
| Decreto 8.420/2015 | Regulates `LAC` federally; state decretos typically mirror it |
| Each state's Lei Orgânica da CGE / Constituição Estadual | State-specific institutional design |

**Caveat**: this file does not enumerate each state's CGE statute
individually. When working on a specific state, start from the
state's CGE website and its founding law.
