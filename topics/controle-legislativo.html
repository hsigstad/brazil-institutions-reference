Controle legislativo — accountability role of Congress

Accountability instruments lodged in the Legislativo: CPIs, the impeachment track (political responsibility), decoro parlamentar mandate-loss proceedings, Senate approval of high appointments, and congressional review of presidential contas. Covers the federal level primarily; state/municipal analogues follow the same grammar through state constitutions and CF.29-A / DL 201/1967.

This file is scoped to Congress-as-accountability-institution, not Congress-as-policy-maker. For coalitional dynamics, party system, and electoral rules, see partidos-e-sistema-eleitoral.md. 1. Comissões Parlamentares de Inquérito (CPIs)

Constitutional and legal basis

  • CF Art. 58 §3: "As comissões parlamentares de inquérito, que terão poderes de investigação próprios das autoridades judiciais, além de outros previstos nos regimentos das respectivas Casas, serão criadas pela Câmara dos Deputados e pelo Senado Federal, em conjunto ou separadamente, mediante requerimento de um terço de seus membros, para a apuração de fato determinado e por prazo certo..."
  • Lei 1.579/1952: Lei das CPIs — general framework for investigation powers and procedure.
  • Regimentos Internos of each house set additional procedural rules.

Requirements

Three cumulative requirements for creation (CF Art. 58 §3):

  1. Requerimento signed by at least 1/3 of the casa's members.
  2. Fato determinado — specific subject matter, not open-ended fishing.
  3. Prazo certo — defined duration (typically 120 days, renewable).

Mixed CPIs (CPMIs) require 1/3 of each house. Historically salient: CPMI dos Correios (2005), CPMI das Milícias Digitais (2023).

Powers and limits

CPIs have "poderes de investigação próprios das autoridades judiciais":

  • Can: quebras de sigilo bancário, fiscal, telefônico (registro, not conteúdo); subpoena witnesses; requisition documents; carry out condução coercitiva (limited post-STF); determine buscas and apreensões in public offices.
  • Cannot: decree prisão (except flagrante); determine interceptação telefônica (reserva jurisdicional); violate foro privilegiado; quebra de sigilo bancário without sufficient fundamentação (STF has tightened review).

STF jurisprudence limits:

  • MS 23.452 (1999) — Celso de Mello's foundational decision on CPI limits; reserva jurisdicional for certain measures.
  • Subsequent HC and MS decisions refined the doctrine, especially around fundamentação of quebras.

Outcomes

CPIs typically end with a relatório final containing conclusões and recommendations (indictment referrals to MP, proposed legislation, administrative measures). A CPI cannot itself impose sanctions — its output is political and evidentiary.

  • Evidentiary value: CPI-produced evidence (testimony, documents) is admissible in subsequent criminal and civil proceedings.
  • Criminal referral: the relatório is routed to the PGR (for federal authorities with foro) or to MPF/MPE for further action.
  • Legislative output: CPIs frequently precede major legislative reforms (e.g., CPI dos Bancos 1999 preceded AML reform; CPMI dos Correios 2005 preceded anti-corruption enforcement intensification).

Empirical caveat

A. Figueiredo (2001) and later work document that a large share of CPIs close without a final report — coalitional majorities often bury CPIs that threaten the government. Selection into CPI creation is politically endogenous, and closure is similarly endogenous.

2. Impeachment and crime de responsabilidade

Federal — President and other high officials

  • Constitutional basis: CF.85 (defines crimes de responsabilidade of the President); CF.86 (procedural framework).
  • Statutory framework: Lei 1.079/1950 — enumerates specific crimes de responsabilidade and the procedure. Received as law by the 1988 CF.

Procedure (CF.86):

  1. Autorização pela Câmara dos Deputados: 2/3 of the Câmara must authorize the processo (CF.86).
  2. Instauração pelo Senado: Senate becomes the impeachment court, presided over by the Chief Justice of the STF (CF.52.I).
  3. Afastamento: President is suspended from office for up to 180 days while the processo runs (CF.86.§1.II).
  4. Condemnação: requires 2/3 of the Senate (CF.52 parágrafo único). Sanction: perda do cargo + inabilitação para função pública por 8 anos.

Historical applications:

  • Fernando Collor de Mello (1992): impeached; resigned moments before Senate vote; Senate condemned anyway, imposing 8-year inabilitação.
  • Dilma Rousseff (2016): impeached and condemned; perda do cargo; Senate vote carved out (inusually) no inabilitação — decision of contested legality but not reviewable judicially.

Other federal officials subject to impeachment (CF.52.II)

The Senate tries ministros do STF, membros do CNJ e CNMP, PGR, and AGU for crimes de responsabilidade. Rare in practice but constitutionally available.

Municipal — prefeitos

  • DL 201/1967: foundational statute on municipal mayors' responsabilidade. Received as law by the 1988 CF.
    • Art. 1 (infrações penais): crimes by prefeitos, tried before TJ (CF.29.X) — not properly "impeachment".
    • Art. 4 (infrações político-administrativas): processo perante a câmara municipal. Quorum: 2/3 for condemnation. Sanction: cassação do mandato.
  • CF.29-A: budget ceiling on poder legislativo municipal spending; unrelated to impeachment per se, but can be the basis for crime de responsabilidade under DL 201 Art. 4.

Historical applications: cassações de prefeitos by câmara sob DL 201 Art. 4 are relatively frequent in small municipalities; rarer in large ones because political costs of deposing a mayor are higher.

State — governors

Each Constituição Estadual sets the procedure, but all mirror the federal model: autorização pela assembleia, julgamento por tribunal misto (desembargadores + deputados estaduais, per STF jurisprudence on state impeachment court composition). Crimes de responsabilidade substantively follow Lei 1.079/1950.

3. Decoro parlamentar and perda de mandato (CF.55)

Grounds for mandate loss

CF.55 lists six grounds for perda do mandato:

  • I: incompatibility violation (CF.54: holding another cargo público, entering into contracts with public bodies, etc.).
  • II: procedimento incompatível com o decoro parlamentar (ethics).
  • III: missing 1/3 of ordinary sessions in a session year.
  • IV: loss or suspension of direitos políticos.
  • V: Justiça Eleitoral decree of perda.
  • VI: condemnação criminal transitada em julgado.

Procedure

  • Grounds I, II, VI: deliberation by the respective casa, maioria absoluta, voto aberto (CF.55.§2, post-EC 76/2013).
  • Grounds III, IV, V: declaration by the casa's mesa diretora, not a political vote.

Conselho de Ética

Each casa has its own Conselho de Ética e Decoro Parlamentar (regulated by internal resolução: e.g., Resolução 25/1993 da Câmara, Resolução 20/1993 do Senado, both amended multiple times).

Process for decoro-based mandate loss:

  1. Representação or iniciativa of the mesa.
  2. Instruction by Conselho de Ética with ampla defesa.
  3. Vote in Conselho → referral to plenary.
  4. Plenary vote (maioria absoluta, CF.55.§2).

Empirical pattern

Decoro-based perdas are rare; most investigated cases end with arquivamento or resignation-before-vote (which preserves political rights). The 2005 mensalão CPI generated only 3 cassações despite dozens of implicated parliamentarians.

4. Senate approval of high appointments

Several constitutionally-significant appointments require Senate approval, which doubles as an accountability function:

  • Ministros do STF: CF.101 sole paragraph — Senate sabatina
    • maioria absoluta.
  • Ministros do STJ, TSE, STM, TST: CF.104 sole paragraph and analogous provisions.
  • PGR: CF.128 §1 — Senate maioria absoluta.
  • Ministros do TCU: CF.73 §2.II — Senate approval of presidential picks (3 of 9).
  • AGU: CF Art. 131 §1 — Senate approval required.
  • BACEN president and directors: post-LC 179/2021 autonomy, all require Senate approval.
  • CGU chief, ANATEL, ANEEL, other agências reguladoras directors: Senate approval under respective statutes.

Sabatina

Before confirmation, each nominee faces a sabatina in the Comissão de Constituição e Justiça (CCJ) do Senado — a public hearing examining qualifications and policy positions. Historically the sabatina has been low-friction: most nominees are approved by large margins, and rejection is rare (but not unprecedented: STF nominee Ney Braga withdrew in 1985; PGR lista tríplice was not Senate-resolved but its bypass by Bolsonaro 2019–2022 drew institutional concern).

Empirical implication: Senate approval is a weak filter on executive appointments in practice; its accountability function is more symbolic than binding.

5. Congressional review of presidential contas (CF.49.IX)

  • CF.49.IX: Congress has competence to "julgar anualmente as contas prestadas pelo Presidente da República".
  • CF.71.I: TCU issues a parecer prévio on presidential accounts within 60 days of receipt.
  • Congress vote: joint session of both houses rejects or approves. Follows TCU recommendation in practice.

Empirical pattern: Congress rarely rejects presidential contas. The first rejection came in 2016 against Dilma's 2015 accounts (related to the pedaladas fiscais question) — which also supplied the substantive basis for the impeachment processo. This illustrates the political dimension of a formally technical review.

6. Overview — congressional accountability relative to other nodes

Congressional accountability instruments share a common weakness in the Power & Taylor (2011) web-of-accountability framework: they depend on political will against the same coalition that gives the executive its working majority. In presidencialismo de coalizão, this creates a structural deficit:

  • CPIs are created only when a 1/3 minority can force them; once created, government majorities can constrain their reach and closure.
  • Impeachment requires 2/3 in both the Câmara (autorização) and the Senate (condemnação) — almost always politically infeasible for a president in a functioning coalition. Collor 1992 and Dilma 2016 both involved prior coalitional collapse, not successful overcoming of an intact coalition.
  • Decoro procedures are majority-controlled; the mesa can stall, the plenary can acquit.
  • Senate approval of appointments has historically been weak.
  • Contas review follows TCU; Congress rarely deviates.

Research-design implication: congressional accountability instruments are strongly selected on political conjuncture. Empirical analyses must control for the underlying coalition configuration rather than treating activations as exogenous signals of accountability performance.
