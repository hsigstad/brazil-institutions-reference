Brazilian Institutions Topics Laws Concepts

Pitfalls — common mistakes about Brazilian institutions

A targeted list of distinctions Claude (or any non-Brazilian reader) is likely to get wrong. Each entry pairs the wrong reading with the correct one and points to the topical file where the distinction is explained in depth. This is not a glossary (see glossario.md for definitions) — it's a list of specific confusions to avoid.

How to use: when in doubt about a Brazilian institutional question, grep this file first. If your interpretation matches one of the "❌" forms below, switch to the corresponding "✓" form.

Civil vs criminal vs administrative

❌ "Improbidade is a criminal offense"

✓ Improbidade is civil (Lei 8.429/92), not criminal

  • Improbidade actions are filed in civil courts (TJ/TRF), not criminal courts.
  • Sanctions are civil-quasi-criminal (loss of office, suspension of political rights, fines, ressarcimento) but not imprisonment.
  • Foro privilegiado does NOT apply to improbidade. A senator accused of improbidade is tried in the ordinary first-instance state court of the comarca where the conduct occurred, not at the STF.
  • The same conduct can also be prosecuted under criminal law in parallel (CP, Lei 8.137/90, Lei 9.613/98 lavagem) — those run in separate proceedings.
  • See topics/improbidade.md §3 ("Procedural framework").

❌ "If a CADE proceeding finds a cartel, the firm has been criminally convicted"

✓ CADE proceedings are administrative

  • CADE imposes administrative fines (Lei 12.529/2011 Art. 37, up to 20% of revenue) under an administrative process.
  • Criminal cartel prosecution runs separately, under LCOT.4 (Lei 8.137/1990 Art. 4), in criminal courts.
  • Improbidade against the public officials who facilitated the cartel is yet another track, in civil courts.
  • A single bid-rigging scheme can generate three parallel proceedings with three different sanction regimes — none of them ne bis in idem because the legal "matters" are distinct.
  • See topics/anticorrupcao-penal.md §7 (parallel prosecution table) and topics/licitacoes.md §"CADE".

MP variants

❌ "MP" or "Ministério Público" as a single national agency

✓ MP is a family of institutions, not one

  • MPF (Ministério Público Federal) — handles federal interests, federal funds, federal officials. Members are Procuradores da República (1st instance, before TRFs).
  • MPE (Ministério Público Estadual) — one per state. Handles state/municipal interests. Members are Promotores de Justiça (1st instance) and Procuradores de Justiça (2nd instance, before TJs). MPE-SP, MPE-RJ, MPE-MG etc. are all separate institutions.
  • MPT (Trabalho), MPM (Militar), MPDFT (DF) — branches of the federal MP umbrella MPU.
  • MPC (de Contas) — operates before Tribunais de Contas (TCU, TCEs), not before judicial courts. Separate concurso, separate career.
  • Improbidade involving municipal funds → MPE files. Improbidade involving federal funds (FPM, SUS, FUNDEB transfers) → MPF.
  • Career movement: a Promotor de Justiça in MPE-SP cannot lateral into MPF without a new concurso. They are different careers.
  • See topics/ministerio-publico.md §2.

Mayoral accounts

❌ "If TCE recommends rejection, the mayor is ineligible"

✓ TCE parecer alone does not trigger Ficha Limpa ineligibility

  • The competent body (per STF Tema 157) is the câmara municipal, not the TCE.
  • TCE issues a parecer prévio; the câmara then votes whether to follow it.
  • Câmara override of the TCE requires a 2/3 supermajority (CF Art. 31 §2).
  • Ineligibility under LI.1.I.g (Ficha Limpa) requires the câmara to vote to reject the accounts. Omission (câmara never votes) also does not generate ineligibility — "inadmissível o julgamento ficto das contas por decurso de prazo".
  • Even when the câmara rejects, the rejection must meet additional cumulative conditions: insanable irregularity, ato doloso, and decisão irrecorrível.
  • See topics/contas-municipais.md §§2–3.

❌ "Contas de governo and contas de gestão are the same thing"

✓ Different categories with different judging bodies

  • Contas de governo = mayor's annual consolidated accounts (political), judged by câmara with TCE assistance, generates Ficha Limpa ineligibility upon rejection.
  • Contas de gestão = mayor's acts as ordenador de despesas (managerial — specific contracts, payments), judged directly by the TCE, can impose débitos and multas, but does not generate Ficha Limpa ineligibility under STF ADPF 982 (~2025).
  • See topics/contas-municipais.md §2.

Súmulas

❌ "TJSP must follow STF súmulas"

✓ Only súmulas vinculantes bind lower courts

  • A regular súmula (issued by STF, STJ, TST, etc.) is a non-binding caselaw summary.
  • Only súmulas vinculantes issued by the STF (CF Art. 103-A, added by EC 45/2004) formally bind lower courts and the administration.
  • A lower court that contradicts an ordinary súmula merely faces appellate reversal; contradicting a súmula vinculante also exposes the judge to reclamação before the STF.
  • See glossario.md ("súmula", "súmula vinculante").

Procedural distinctions

❌ "Conexão and prevenção mean the same thing"

✓ Different concepts

  • Conexão = legal connection between cases that justifies joint handling (NCPC Arts. 55–57).
  • Prevenção = the consequence of being the first judge to handle a related matter; that judge becomes the julgador certo for all subsequent acts in the same processo.
  • Conexão is the substantive criterion; prevenção is the procedural result.

❌ "Impedimento and suspeição are interchangeable"

✓ Different in legal nature

  • Impedimento is matéria de ordem pública — the judge can recognize it ex officio at any stage. Grounds are objective (e.g., judge is a relative of a party).
  • Suspeição is not matéria de ordem pública — it must be raised by a party. Grounds are subjective (e.g., judge has expressed an opinion on the case publicly).
  • See glossario.md ("impedimento", "suspeição").

❌ "All STF decisions are súmulas vinculantes"

✓ Most STF decisions are NOT binding precedents

  • Súmulas vinculantes are a small subset (~60 issued total since EC 45/2004).
  • STF jurisprudence in Temas de repercussão geral (RE/ARE) binds lower courts via the repercussão geral mechanism — these are also binding but not the same as súmulas vinculantes.
  • ADI / ADC / ADPF decisions bind everyone (eficácia erga omnes).
  • Other STF decisions (e.g., individual HCs, individual REs without repercussão geral) bind only the parties.

Foro privilegiado

❌ "All cases involving politicians go to the STF"

✓ Foro privilegiado is narrow and case-type specific

  • Applies to criminal cases involving certain offices (presidente, vice, ministros, parlamentares federais, governadores, etc.) under CF Art. 102.
  • After STF AP 937 (2018), it applies only to crimes committed in office and in connection with the office.
  • Improbidade is civil and does not carry foro privilegiado.
  • Civil actions in general do not carry foro privilegiado.
  • Most state-level officeholders (vereadores, prefeitos, etc.) do NOT have foro privilegiado at the STF. Some have foro at the TJ for criminal cases (e.g., prefeitos at TJ).
  • See topics/procedimentos-legais.md.

Electoral procedures

❌ "AIME, AIJE, and AIPRC are interchangeable ways to remove a candidate"

✓ Different deadlines, different evidentiary standards, different effects

  • AIPRC (Ação de Impugnação ao Pedido de Registro de Candidatura) — before the election. Challenges the candidate's registration. Filed within 5 days of the registration request, with hard cutoff at 15 August. → topics/processo-eleitoral.md §4.
  • AIJE (Ação de Investigação Judicial Eleitoral) — during the campaign. Investigates abuse of economic/political/media power. Filed between candidate registration and diplomação. → topics/processo-eleitoral.md §6.
  • AIME (Ação de Impugnação de Mandato Eletivo) — after the election. Revokes the mandate of an elected candidate based on corruption, abuse of economic power, or fraude. 15-day deadline after diplomação. CF Art. 14 §10. Segredo de justiça. → topics/processo-eleitoral.md §5.
  • RCED (Recurso Contra a Expedição do Diploma) — also after the election. Challenges the diploma based on supervening or constitutional ineligibility. 3-day deadline after diplomação. → topics/processo-eleitoral.md §7.
  • AIJE and AIME generate inelegibilidade (8 years) only for abuse of economic power, not for corrupção or fraude alone.

❌ "Coligações still exist for federal deputy elections"

✓ Coligações for proportional elections were abolished by EC 97/2017

  • Pre-2020: parties could form electoral coalitions for proportional elections (deputado federal, deputado estadual, vereador). Coligações pooled votes for the quociente eleitoral.
  • Post-2020 (EC 97/2017): coligações for proportional elections are prohibited. Only federações partidárias (Lei 14.208/2021) are permitted, and they require a multi-year commitment.
  • Coligações for majoritário offices remain legal (President, Governor, Prefeito, Senador).
  • Any pre-2020 vs post-2020 comparison must account for this.
  • See topics/partidos-e-sistema-eleitoral.md §3.

Labor courts

❌ "Brazilian workers always file labor cases for free"

✓ Pre-2017 vs post-2017 are fundamentally different regimes

  • Pre-November 2017: zero filing costs, zero loser-pays attorney fees in labor courts. Justiça gratuita automatic for workers earning up to 2× minimum wage.
  • Post-November 2017 (Lei 13.467/17): 2% filing costs, 5–15% honorários de sucumbência, expert fees on the losing party, no-show penalty even for justiça gratuita beneficiaries.
  • STF ADI 5766 (October 2021): partially struck down the harshest cost provisions for justiça gratuita beneficiaries. Restored a partial pre-2017 protection.
  • Filing volume dropped ~34% in 2018 vs 2017 — the sharpest filing shock in any major federal cost reform.
  • See topics/justica-trabalho.md.

Procurement

❌ "Lei 8.666/93 is the current procurement law"

✓ Lei 14.133/2021 is the current law; transition ended 30 Dec 2023

  • Lei 8.666 governed Brazilian public procurement from 1993 to 2023.
  • Lei 14.133/2021 (Nova Lei de Licitações) replaces Lei 8.666, Lei 10.520/2002 (pregão), and Lei 12.462/2011 (RDC).
  • Transition period: municipalities could choose old or new regime until 30 December 2023 (L14133.191). After that date, Lei 14.133 is mandatory.
  • For historical analysis (pre-2024 conduct), Lei 8.666 is the relevant statute. For current operations and any post-2024 conduct, Lei 14.133.
  • See topics/licitacoes.md.

❌ "Pregão is the same as concorrência"

✓ Different modalities with different procedures

  • Concorrência is one of the five Lei 8.666/93 modalities (an open public auction for high-value contracts).
  • Pregão is a separate modality created by Lei 10.520/2002 — a reverse auction (descending price). Originally for goods and standard services.
  • Pregão eletrônico has been mandatory for the federal government since Decreto 10.024/2019 and is the dominant modality in practice.
  • Lei 14.133/2021 absorbs both into the same statute but preserves the distinction.
  • See topics/licitacoes.md.

Federalism

❌ "Most Brazilian municipalities collect their own tax revenue"

✓ Most municipalities are transfer-dependent

  • The median Brazilian municipality raises less than 10% of total revenue from own-source taxes (IPTU, ISS, ITBI, taxas).
  • The bulk of municipal revenue comes from constitutional transfers: FPM (federal), ICMS quota-parte (state), FUNDEB (education), SUS transfers (health), royalties.
  • This is why the FPM step function is a canonical RD instrument — small municipalities are extremely sensitive to FPM coefficient changes.
  • See topics/federalismo-fiscal.md §2 and §3.

❌ "FPM is distributed proportionally to population"

✓ FPM is distributed by population brackets (a step function)

  • The Decreto-Lei 1.881/1981 + LC 62/1989 set 16 population brackets for interior municipalities. Each bracket has a fixed coefficient.
  • A municipality crossing a bracket boundary experiences a discrete jump in FPM per capita.
  • This step function is used as an RD design in the empirical literature (Litschig & Morrison; Brollo–Nannicini–Perotti–Tabellini; many others).
  • See topics/federalismo-fiscal.md §2 and quasi-experimentos.md.

CGU

❌ "CGU is the same as TCU"

✓ Different control bodies with different constitutional positions

  • CGU (Controladoria-Geral da União) is controle interno — it belongs to the federal Executivo and audits federal money from inside the executive branch.
  • TCU (Tribunal de Contas da União) is controle externo — it reports to Congress and operates independently of the executive (CF Art. 71).
  • A given irregularity in federal funds can be pursued by both CGU (internal control, can refer to MP) and TCU (external control, binding decisions on administrators).
  • See topics/cgu-controle-interno.md and topics/tribunais-contas.md.

❌ "CGU random audits are still running"

✓ The random lottery program ended in 2015

  • The Programa de Fiscalização por Sorteios (PFS) ran 2003–2015, ~40 lotteries, with public random selection of municipalities.
  • After 2015, the program was reformulated as the Programa de Fiscalização em Entes Federativos, which uses risk-based / thematic selection instead of random lottery.
  • The random-assignment property applies strictly to 2003–2015. Post-2015 audits require different identification assumptions.
  • See topics/cgu-controle-interno.md §3 and quasi-experimentos.md.

STF jurisprudence

❌ "Tema 1199 abolished culposa improbidade entirely, including for past convictions"

✓ Tema 1199 makes the elimination of culposa irretroactive in part

  • The 2021 LIA reform (Lei 14.230/2021) eliminated culposa improbidade.
  • STF Tema 1199 (ARE 843.989, August 2022) held:
    • For cases with trânsito em julgado: the elimination is irretroactive — past culposa convictions stand.
    • For pending cases without final judgment: the new law applies; courts must re-examine intent and dismiss if only negligence is proven.
  • The new 8-year prescription rule is also irretroactive — for pre-reform conduct, the 8-year clock starts from 26 October 2021 (the law's publication date).
  • See topics/improbidade.md §2 and jurisprudencia-stf.md §1.

❌ "Tema 157 says the câmara has the final word on all mayoral conduct"

✓ Tema 157 is about Ficha Limpa ineligibility, not all sanctions

  • Tema 157 (RE 848.826, August 2016) holds that for purposes of Ficha Limpa, the câmara is the órgão competente for both contas de governo and contas de gestão.
  • ADPF 982 (~2025) clarified that TCEs can still independently judge contas de gestão for non-Ficha-Limpa purposes (impose débitos and multas).
  • The câmara controls the electoral consequence (inelegibilidade); the TCE controls the administrative consequence (financial liability and sanctions).
  • See topics/contas-municipais.md §2 and jurisprudencia-stf.md §2.

Citations and database lookups

❌ "Lei 8.429/1992 Art. 9 is the canonical citation form"

✓ Use the compact apelido form: LIA.9

  • The canonical backtick form for cataloged laws uses the apelido, not the long name. LIA is the apelido for Lei 8.429/1992.
  • Long form Lei 13.709/2018 Art. 7 is reserved for non-cataloged laws (and even then, the canonical fonte_id form L13709-2018.7 is preferred).
  • See CLAUDE.md "Citing statutes" section and leis_index.yaml for the apelido catalog.

❌ "An empty result from cite.py means the law doesn't exist"

✓ It may mean the law isn't cataloged, or the path is wrong

  • Only ~50 laws are currently cataloged in institutions.db. Many important laws (CLT, EC amendments, sectoral statutes) are not yet in the article DB. Their citations parse but don't resolve.
  • An empty result for a cataloged law means either (a) the path doesn't exist in that article (try the article without the path to see what's available), (b) the law's parser couldn't extract that level of granularity, or (c) the article number/letter is wrong.
  • For laws not in the catalog, fall back to planalto.gov.br with the same article+path.
  • See tools/leis_artigos/README.md for the catalog and lookup instructions.

Empirical research traps

❌ "Pre/post-2017 labor court filings are comparable"

✓ The 2017 reform (Lei 13.467) changed cost rules — filing rates dropped ~35%

  • Before Nov 2017: workers filed for free (justiça gratuita near-universal).
  • After: losing parties may owe honorários de sucumbência and perícia costs (though STF ADI5766 struck down part of these provisions in Oct 2021).
  • Any time series crossing Nov 2017 has a structural break that is a cost effect, not a change in underlying disputes. → topics/justica-trabalho.md

❌ "Post-2024 court congestion improved because courts got more efficient"

✓ CNJ Res. 547/2024 extinguished >10M execuções fiscais administratively

  • Execuções fiscais ≤R$10,000 were mass-extinguished on economic-unviability grounds. Stock fell from ~27M to ~16.5M in <2 years.
  • Any pre/post-2024 comparison of congestion rates, pending-case counts, or average-duration statistics is contaminated by this administrative cleanup. → topics/cnj-administracao-judicial.md §7

❌ "Conviction rate = enforcement rate"

✓ The same conduct generates parallel proceedings — specify which regime

  • A single corruption scheme can appear simultaneously in criminal court (CP/LCOT), civil court (LIA improbidade), administrative tribunal (CADE), CGU (Lei Anticorrupção), TCE (audit), and electoral justice (Ficha Limpa). Each has different conviction/sanction rates.
  • "What share of corruption cases result in conviction?" is meaningless without specifying the regime. → topics/anticorrupcao-penal.md §7

❌ "Pre-2020 and post-2020 proportional election results are directly comparable"

✓ EC 97/2017 banned coligações in proportional elections from 2020 onward

  • Under coligações, a popular candidate in party A "carried" weak candidates from party B into office via pooled vote totals. Post-2020, each party (or federação) stands alone. The mapping from votes to seats changed structurally. → topics/partidos-e-sistema-eleitoral.md §3

❌ "LAI requests always existed — I can study transparency across all years"

✓ LAI took effect May 2012 — pre-2012 data availability is structurally different

  • Before LAI, access to public data depended on ad-hoc requests and institutional goodwill. After May 2012, agencies must respond within 20 days with a formal appeal chain (CGU for federal executive).
  • Projects spanning this boundary should account for measurement differences. → topics/transparencia-dados.md §1

❌ "Execução fiscal cases represent real judicial work"

✓ ~34% of pending cases are execuções fiscais — mostly dormant

  • Execuções fiscais (tax enforcement) are ~34% of all pending cases and have an ~88% congestion rate. Most sit dormant waiting for asset location. Including them in aggregate productivity or duration statistics dramatically skews the picture. → topics/cnj-administracao-judicial.md §7

When in doubt

If you're about to make a confident-sounding claim about Brazilian institutions and it isn't anchored in one of the topical files, the leis_index.yaml, or jurisprudencia-stf.md, stop and verify. The cost of being wrong on Brazilian institutional questions is high (legal claims, dates, who has standing, what triggers what) and the files are designed so the right answer is one grep away.
